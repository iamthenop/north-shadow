# Castles Do Not Float
### Part II: The Dream

For most of history, systems were bound to the ground.

They occupied buildings. Machines had to be installed, maintained, and eventually replaced.  
Operations required people who understood where the machinery lived and how it behaved.

Infrastructure had weight.

---
## Abstraction

Then abstraction began to change everything.

Servers became instances.  
Networks became services.  
Storage became an API.

Infrastructure did not disappear, but it became easier to use. The machinery moved further away from the people building systems.

---
## Elastic Systems

New capabilities followed.

Systems could scale without building new data centers.  
Applications could deploy in minutes instead of months.  
Entire platforms could be created from configuration alone.

Infrastructure began to feel lighter.

---
## The Cloud

This was the promise of the cloud.

Organizations no longer needed to own the machines that powered their systems.  
They could focus on services instead of hardware.  
Teams could build faster than ever before.

The cloud did not remove infrastructure.

But it made infrastructure easier to reach.

---
## Acceleration

The impact was immediate.

Startups could launch without capital-intensive infrastructure.  
Large organizations could modernize systems that had taken decades to build.  
Developers gained the ability to deploy services across the world.

The pace of innovation accelerated.

---
## A New Perspective

Something subtle changed as well.

When infrastructure becomes distant, it becomes easier to forget that it exists.

From the perspective of the people building systems, the machinery had vanished.

What remained was the platform.

And from that vantage point, the castle appeared to rise into the clouds.
