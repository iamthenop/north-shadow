# Part III: A Basket of Kittens
### Mr. Whiskerson

## Seen

For a while, the second cat remained easy to absorb into the background.

It came and went. Mr. Whiskerson came and went. The lake had its usual rhythm of partial visibility. Things appeared, disappeared, and returned without explanation.

Then there were kittens.

Not a possibility anymore. Not a question of where the second cat had come from. Kittens could be gathered. They could be carried. They could be placed somewhere visible.

There was a basket of kittens.

## Provision

The first response was not alarm.

It was care.

Food began to appear in larger amounts. Before long, a small cat house appeared as well. None of it needed to be framed as a larger decision. The kittens were there. Food was needed. Shelter was needed.

That was enough.

## What Made It Easy

The kittens did what kittens do.

They climbed over each other and fell asleep in a pile. They wandered only a few feet before turning back. They treated the basket like a border and the little shelter like an answer. When food appeared, they crowded around it with the kind of trust that makes care feel not only necessary, but deserved.

That was part of what made it hard to call any of it a problem.

What was fragile could still look lively. What was unsustainable could still look warm. A small shelter, a bulk bag of food, and a visible place to gather were enough to make the whole arrangement feel more stable than it was.

For a while, that feeling held.

## What Was Said

The reasoning around it stayed familiar.

Cats were resilient. Mr. Whiskerson had been there for years. Things around the lake had a way of sorting themselves out. Not everything that appeared had to become a larger problem.

Alongside that was another boundary.

They could not be kept.

That much remained important.

But food was there now.

Shelter was there now.
