# Castles Do Not Float
### Part II: The Dream

For most of history, systems were bound to the ground.

They occupied buildings. Machines had to be installed, maintained, and eventually replaced. Operations required people who understood where the machinery lived and how it behaved.

Infrastructure had weight.

## Abstraction

Then abstraction began to change how systems were built.

Servers became instances. Networks became services. Storage became an API.

Infrastructure did not disappear, but it became easier to use. The machinery moved further away from the people building systems, and with that distance came a different way of thinking about what infrastructure was.

It no longer felt like a physical constraint first.

It felt like a capability.

## Elastic Systems

New capabilities followed.

Systems could scale without building new data centers. Applications could deploy in minutes instead of months. Entire platforms could be created from configuration alone.

What once required procurement, coordination, and lead time could now be absorbed into the development process itself.

Infrastructure began to feel lighter.

## The Cloud

This was the promise of the cloud.

Organizations no longer needed to own the machines that powered their systems. They could focus on services instead of hardware. Teams could build faster than ever before without carrying the same visible operational burden.

The cloud did not remove infrastructure.

It changed how infrastructure was reached, and how it was experienced.

## Acceleration

The impact was immediate.

Startups could launch without capital-intensive infrastructure. Large organizations could modernize systems that had taken decades to build. Developers gained the ability to deploy services across the world without first building the machinery that made those services possible.

The pace of innovation accelerated, and with it, expectations changed. What had once been considered difficult became routine.

## A New Perspective

Something subtler changed as well.

As infrastructure became more distant, it became easier to forget that it existed at all. Systems still ran, but the machinery beneath them no longer appeared in the ordinary rhythm of building and operating software.

From the perspective of the builder, the machinery had effectively vanished.

What remained was the platform.

And from that vantage point, the castle appeared to rise into the clouds.
