## Part I: Edible

### The pot

The power had gone out sometime before dawn.

By late afternoon, the work had moved to the shed. The wood stove was already hot. A heavy pot sat on the flat top with venison, potatoes, and stock going slowly together. From a distance, the arrangement looked complete.

The first spoonful said otherwise.

The pot had heat. The pot had time. The pot had substance. The broth carried meat and potato together without binding them. The flavors remained adjacent. The stew stayed separate all the way to the bottom.

Only then did the omission become obvious.

No onions. No bell peppers. No celery.

The base had never gone in.

### Upward corrections

That changed the meaning of every correction that followed.

More salt helped one spoonful. Pepper helped another. Garlic powder made the next taste busier. Tomato paste gave the broth color and the suggestion of depth. Each addition changed the surface. None of them corrected the structure.

Hunger made another correction seem reasonable.

The pot remained edible. The meal remained serviceable. Nothing had collapsed. Nothing had become dramatic. But the base never entered the system, and later additions could not recover what early conditions failed to establish.

Some conditions must be corrected before the system begins.

### Surface and structure

The same pattern appears in systems.

Once the base is wrong, most work moves upward. More controls. More evidence. More visible order. Some of these additions help in narrow ways. None of them can provide a structural condition that was never built in.

The surface changed. The structure did not.

That is the easier kind of failure to miss. A system can remain live, visible, and technically serviceable while lacking the conditions that would make it fit to serve. Reports can stay clean. Controls can remain present. Activity can continue. From a distance, the system can look coherent.

Edible is not the same as fit to serve.
