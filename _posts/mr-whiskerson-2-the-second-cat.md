# Part II: The Second Cat
### Mr. Whiskerson

## Another One

For years, it had just been Mr. Whiskerson.

He had been around long enough that no one thought very hard about him anymore. He moved through the same yards and docks along the lake, showed up when he felt like it, and disappeared again without leaving much behind.

Then another cat started showing up.

It did not arrive in any dramatic way. No one found it. No one brought it over. It was just there one day, and then there again after that.

At first, it did not seem especially important.

Cats wandered through from time to time. Sometimes they belonged to someone a few places over. Sometimes they stayed for a bit and then moved on. A single new cat was still easy to file under the same kind of ordinary uncertainty that already surrounded Mr. Whiskerson.

No one expected another cat, and no one ever really figured out where it came from.

There were guesses, of course. It probably belonged to someone farther down the lake. It probably came over from another stretch of shoreline. It probably would not stay.

Those explanations were enough for the moment.

## Still Fine

Mr. Whiskerson did not stop being familiar just because there was another cat.

He still came and went the way he always had. He still showed up on the deck. He still passed through without causing trouble. The second cat did not immediately change any of that. It only made the edges of the situation a little less clear.

But not by much.

There was still no mess to clean up. Still no noise at night. Still no obvious reason to call anyone or do anything about it.

It was just one more cat.

Every now and then someone would notice the two of them in roughly the same places and ask the obvious question. Where had this one come from?

No one had a real answer.

And because there was no answer, people reached for the easier possibilities instead. It belonged somewhere. It had wandered over. It would move on.

Nothing about the setting pushed back very hard against those explanations. Around the lake, things drift in and out of view all the time. Things show up, linger for a while, and then disappear again. Another cat still fit inside that kind of thinking.

So nothing was treated as urgent.

No one changed how they thought about Mr. Whiskerson. No one went back to check what had already been assumed. No one felt much pressure to turn a vague concern into a real question.

For the time being, it was still possible to look at the whole thing and think nothing had really changed.

There were two cats now.
